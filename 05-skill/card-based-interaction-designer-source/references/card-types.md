# Card Type Library

Use these formats to shape a deck. Choose formats that match the deck purpose, audience, setting, and disclosure level.

## Low-risk choice formats

### Pick one
Use for fast engagement, preferences, priorities, light debate, or values by proxy.
Pattern: Pick one option from a short list. Why?
Avoid when a single choice oversimplifies a real decision.

### Would you rather
Use for trade-offs and friendly debate.
Pattern: Would you rather A or B? Why?
Avoid options that make people reveal private needs or real workplace grievances.

### Pick 2 of 5
Use for prioritisation and trade-off thinking.
Pattern: Pick 2 from 5 options. Why those two?
Good for change support, values, capability building, and family games.

### Rank them
Use for sorting priorities and comparing assumptions.
Pattern: Rank these from most to least useful / risky / important.
Avoid ranking people or teams.

### This or that, then why
Use for simple warm-up cards.
Pattern: A or B? Why?
Keep options balanced so neither answer sounds morally better.

## Scenario formats

### What would you do?
Use for workplace training, change management, ethics, customer situations, safety, and leadership practice.
Pattern: In this situation, what would you do first?
Keep the scenario realistic and non-accusatory.

### Decision fork
Use for competing paths.
Pattern: You can do A or B. What do you choose and why?
Good for strategic and operational discussions.

### Spot the risk
Use for capability, process, and change-readiness decks.
Pattern: Which risk is most likely here? What would reduce it?
Avoid making the risk point at a real person.

### Myth or fact
Use for training and misconception correction.
Pattern: Myth or fact? Explain the answer.
Include answer guidance if the deck is instructional.

### Explain it to someone new
Use for teach-back, onboarding, and AI adoption.
Pattern: Explain this idea to a new starter or teenager in plain language.

## Action and reflection formats

### Start, stop, continue
Use for retrospectives, team norms, personal growth, and improvement.
Avoid using it to pressure public criticism.

### Keep, change, remove
Use for process improvement and change workshops.
Pattern: What should we keep, change, or remove?
Use anonymous capture if the context is sensitive.

### Tiny experiment
Use for behaviour change and practical next steps.
Pattern: What is one small test we could try this week?
Good for change adoption.

### Support kit
Use for change, training, personal growth, and resilience.
Pattern: Pick 2 supports that would help someone succeed.
This is safer than asking what someone fears.

### Future headline
Use for vision, planning, and culture.
Pattern: A year from now, what headline would you like to see?
Keep it realistic enough to be useful.

### Confidence scale
Use for readiness checks.
Pattern: Choose a confidence level and one thing that would move it up.
For workplaces, avoid asking people to explain low confidence publicly.

## Group and role formats

### Build your team
Use for roles, strengths, planning, and playful group interaction.
Avoid assigning negative roles to named people.

### Choose your role
Use for workshops and training.
Pattern: In this situation, which role would you take?

### Build your kit
Use for practical readiness and support needs.
Pattern: Choose tools, supports, resources, or behaviours.
Good for change management and family game decks.

### One rule
Use for values, norms, and playful governance.
Pattern: You can make one rule for this situation. What is it?
Avoid real policy decisions unless the activity is explicitly consultative.

## Wildcard formats

### Power with a catch
Use for fun, creativity, and disguised trade-off thinking.
Pattern: You get a benefit, but there is a downside. Take it or leave it?

### Wild card
Use for novelty and energy shifts.
Keep it safe and optional.

### Pass and redirect
Use in sensitive decks.
Pattern: Pass, answer privately, or answer the lighter version.
